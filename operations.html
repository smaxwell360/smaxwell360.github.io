When Vocus went viral following the launch of a new marketing SaaS product, daily press releases submissions exploded from 300 to over 2,300 — an 820% surge that a team of 5–7 simply couldn't absorb. Domestic hiring alone wasn't going to close the gap fast enough, and 24/7 global coverage was a requirement, not a nice-to-have. I made the case for building a 40-person remote operation in the Philippines, personally owning the training curriculum to ensure Manila output was indistinguishable from US output — not close, indistinguishable. I built weekly QA audits with structured comparisons across the same submission types, required rework when output missed the bar, and fed every pattern back into training. Simultaneously, I scaled the US team from 5 to 15 using a structured mentorship model built by top performers. The result: a global team of 60+ that sustained the volume increase without a single degradation in quality scores, 24/7 coverage for international customers, and a Manila operation that became the most efficient part of the entire global team — with junior hires eventually moving into senior leadership roles.

Maryland needed a statewide COVID-19 contact tracing platform in four months, with no playbook, shifting state requirements, and an engineering team already at capacity. I sat between state government and a technical team and owned everything: scrum facilitation, stakeholder alignment, training, and onboarding for all 1,700 users across 26 local health departments. Mid-development, senior state officials pushed to add a complex case management suite — a scope change that my engineering velocity data showed would blow the deadline or ship an unstable product. Rather than just pushing back, I built an explicit trade-off document and reframed the conversation: this wasn't a "no," it was a sequencing decision. We'd nail the core platform for launch and deliver case management as a fast-follow in Phase 2. The platform launched on every critical deadline, achieved 97% user adoption within 30 days, restored the 48-hour contact tracing window that public health depended on, and two struggling local health departments cleared their backlogs within the first week. Phase 2 was delivered on a more stable foundation — and we came out with more trust from state leadership than we started with.

Mainstay  ·  AI SaaS & Enterprise Operations

Rolling out AI automation across a 29-plant enterprise in 4 weeks

74%Autonomous resolution at launch
99%Accuracy through iteration
29Plants deployed
~60%Faster than standard process

GREIF, a global industrial packaging manufacturer, needed Mainstay's AI chatbot deployed across 29 factory locations within a 6-month pilot window — but our standard implementation process averaged 2–3 months of discovery per location. The math didn't work, plant managers were unavailable, and renegotiating the timeline wasn't an option. I recognized that the standard discovery model was wrong for this constraint. Instead of waiting on location-specific input that would never arrive, I deployed a generalized "Seed" knowledge base simultaneously across all 29 plants — covering core company information — and used incoming employee queries from day one as live discovery data. I monitored message logs across all locations in parallel, patched the highest-frequency gaps first, and ignored edge cases in favor of the 80% that mattered most (call-out procedures, supervisor contacts, shift schedules). All 29 plants were live in 4 weeks — roughly 60% faster than the standard process — with a 74% autonomous resolution rate at launch. Through continued iteration, accuracy climbed to 99%. The approach directly contributed to $1.5M ARR and became the repeatable multi-site deployment playbook for all future enterprise launches.
